


1. High-Energy Pop vs. Chill Lofi

These two profiles are basically complete opposites, and the results reflect that. The pop profile brings up fast, upbeat, polished songs, while the lofi profile gives slower, calmer, more acoustic tracks. What’s interesting is that both profiles scored their top songs almost the same (around 5.9/6), which shows the system is pretty balanced. It rewards high energy for high-energy users and low energy for low-energy users equally, so it’s not biased toward one side.


2. Chill Lofi vs. Deep Intense Rock

The lofi profile had three songs from its genre in the top three, while the rock profile only had one. This isn’t really a problem with the algorithm—it’s more of a data issue. There are just more lofi songs in the catalog than rock songs. So the lofi user gets better variety, while the rock user doesn’t have much to choose from. It shows how the dataset itself can create bias, even if the scoring system is fair.


3. Deep Intense Rock vs. EDGE: Sad but High-Energy

Both of these profiles want high energy, but they get very different results. The rock profile gets songs that actually feel intense, while the sad-but-high-energy blues profile gets a slow, sad song ranked first just because the genre and mood labels match. This shows that the system prioritizes labels over how the song actually feels. The rock profile gets what it asked for, but the blues profile exposes a flaw where conflicting preferences aren’t handled well.


4. EDGE: Genre Not in Catalog vs. EDGE: All Mid-Range Values

Both of these profiles don’t really benefit from the genre bonus. The missing-genre user (like bossa nova) actually does okay because their preferences are close to jazz or lofi songs. But the mid-range user struggles more. Since most songs are either clearly high or low energy, someone with average preferences (around 0.5) doesn’t match anything well. So their recommendations feel kind of off. This shows the dataset is a bit polarized and doesn’t serve “middle” users as well.

5.  Why Gym Hero Keeps Showing Up for Happy Pop Users

Gym Hero keeps showing up because, on paper, it checks almost every box. It’s pop (so it gets the genre bonus), it has really high energy, and it sounds bright overall. The only mismatch is that it’s labeled “intense” instead of “happy,” which drops its score slightly. That’s why it usually ranks second instead of first. But realistically, “intense pop” and “happy pop” feel pretty different—one is more like a workout song, the other is more feel-good. The system sees them as very similar, but a real listener would probably notice the difference right away.
